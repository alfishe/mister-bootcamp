# MiSTer Audio Pipeline Architecture

The audio pipeline in the MiSTer framework is responsible for taking digital audio signals generated by the FPGA cores and delivering them to the end user with high fidelity and minimal latency. The system supports both analog and digital audio paths simultaneously.

---

## 1. High-Level Architecture

The audio subsystem bridges the output from the specific emulated hardware (the "Core") to the physical outputs on the DE10-Nano and the I/O Board.

```mermaid

        A_MIX --> |L/R Digital Audio| R[Right Channel]

        LPF --> |3.5mm / RCA| ANALOG_OUT[Analog Speaker/Headphones]
    end
```

## 2. Core Audio Generation

Inside the FPGA core, retro audio chips (e.g., Yamaha FM synthesizers, AY-3-8910, SID) are simulated using cycle-accurate logic. 

- **Sample Rates**: Many vintage systems generate audio at non-standard sample rates (e.g., 3.58 MHz / 64). The core typically handles any necessary resampling to output a standard rate.
- **Bit Depth**: Core audio output is generally delivered to the `sys` framework as 16-bit or 24-bit signed integers for the Left and Right channels.
- **Volume Control**: OSD-based volume control is often applied before the signal leaves the core, scaling the digital values.

---

## 3. The Digital Path: I2S and HDMI

For high-fidelity digital output, MiSTer utilizes the **I2S (Inter-IC Sound)** protocol.

### 3.1 I2S Transmitter
The `sys` framework includes an I2S transmitter module that serializes the parallel audio data from the core.
- **Standardization**: The audio is typically transmitted at **48 kHz** or **96 kHz**, which is standard for HDMI audio.
- **Signals**: It generates the required I2S signals:
    - `LRCLK` (Left/Right Clock or Word Select)
    - `BCLK` (Bit Clock)
    - `SDATA` (Serial Data)

### 3.2 HDMI Audio
The I2S stream is routed directly to the HDMI transmitter chip (e.g., ADV7513 on the DE10-Nano) or embedded into the video blanking intervals if using a direct FPGA-to-HDMI implementation. This ensures a pristine, lossless digital audio signal.

### 3.3 SPDIF (Optical/Coaxial)
If the user has an I/O board with a digital audio out (TOSLINK), the same digital stream is formatted for SPDIF transmission.

---

## 4. The Analog Path: Sigma-Delta Modulation

For analog audio output (the 3.5mm jack on the I/O board), MiSTer does not use a traditional external DAC (Digital-to-Analog Converter) chip for the primary audio output to save costs and reduce latency. Instead, it leverages the FPGA itself using **Sigma-Delta ($\Sigma-\Delta$) Modulation**.

### 4.1 How it Works
1.  **Oversampling**: The 16/24-bit digital audio signal is highly oversampled inside the FPGA.
2.  **Pulse Width Modulation (PWM)**: The Sigma-Delta modulator converts the high-resolution digital signal into a very high-frequency, 1-bit bitstream (PWM). 
3.  **Noise Shaping**: The modulator pushes quantization noise up into high frequency bands, far beyond the range of human hearing.

### 4.2 The Analog Filter
The high-frequency 1-bit signal exits the FPGA via standard GPIO pins. On the MiSTer I/O Board, this signal passes through a simple **RC Low-Pass Filter (LPF)**.
- The filter acts as a physical integrator, smoothing the high-frequency PWM pulses into a continuous analog waveform.
- Because the noise was shifted to high frequencies by the Sigma-Delta modulator, the low-pass filter easily removes it, leaving clean, high-quality analog audio.

---

## 5. Timing and Synchronization

Audio synchronization is critical to prevent buffer underruns (crackling) or overruns (dropped frames).
- **Video-Driven Audio**: In many cores, the audio generation is synchronized to the video frame rate. If the video runs at 59.94Hz, the audio must be paced to match that exact frame duration to keep the HDMI sink perfectly synchronized.
- **Resampling**: If the core's native speed deviates significantly from standard refresh rates, a high-quality Sample Rate Converter (SRC) module may be used in the `sys` framework to align the audio stream with the 48kHz HDMI requirement.
